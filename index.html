Chapter 1. You Can't Always Trust the Numbers

  • Financial experts have a history of "cooking" their books, through both licit and illicit means.
  • "As long as there are liars and thieves on this earth [...] [there will be] fraud and embezzlement." (Berman & Knight, 2013, p.3)
  • One "legitimate" technique is a one-time charge, which allows a company to take a collection of bad news and cram it into one quarter's financial results so that subsequent quarters look better.
  • Another "legitimate" technique is whereby a company repositions various expenses from one category to another to "pretty up" an earnings picture to boost its stock price.
  • Bookkeepers cannot know exactly what everyone in the company does, how long equipment will last, etc. so they do not know how to perfectly allocate costs in a given period.
  • The art of acccounting and finance is the art of using limited data to come as close as possile to an accurate description of how well a company is performing.
  • Income Statement: The income statement is a financial statement that shows revenues, expenses, and profit for a period of time; such as a quarter, month, or year. The statement can also be called a profit and loss (P&L) statement, statement of earnings, or statement of operations. Sometimes the word consolidated is used in the aforementioned terms. The bottom line is the statement gives net profit, also known as net income or net earnings.
  • Operating Expenses: Operating expenses refers to the costs that are required to keep the business operating day-to-day. These costs include: salaries, benefits, and insurance costs, among other items. Operating expenses are on the income statement and are subtracted from revenue to determine the net profit.
  • Capital Expenditures: Capital expenditures refers to the purchase of an item that is considered a long-term investment, such as a computer system or equipment. Most companies consider any purhcase over a certian dollar amount a capital expenditure, while anything less is an operating expense.
  • The two disciplines are not reality, but a reflection of reality. The accuracy of that reflection depends on the ability of the accountants and finance professionals to make reasonable assumptions and calculate reasonable estimates." (Berman & Knight, 2013, p.5)
  • Operating expenses show up on the income statement, whereas capital expenditures show up on the balance sheet - reducing profit, so only a piece of the depreciating asset shows up on the income statement.
  • The result of these assumptions is typically a bias in the numbers, that is to say the data is skewed in one direction or another, and even more so in larger and larger datasets.
  • One rule, however, is clear, and that is: Revenue is recorded when it is earned, meaning the product or service is delivered.
  • A second rule is operating expense reduces the bottom line immediately, whereas capital expenditure spreads the hit out over several accounting periods.
  • Point of the chapter: If you aren't aware of the assumptions and estimates that underline the numbers and how those assumptions and estimates affect the numbers in one direction or another, your understanding may be faulty and/or naive.

Chapter 2. Spotting Assumptions, Estimates, and Biases

  • Accountants use both accruals and allocations to try to create an accurate picture of the business for the month.
  • Accrual: An accrual is the portion of a given revenue or expense item that is recorded in a particiular time span. For instance, product development costs are likely to be spread out over several accounting periods, and so a portion of the total cost will be accrued each month. The purpose of accruals is to match revenues to costs in a given time period as accurately as possible. (Berman & Knight, 2013, p.11)
  • Allocation: Allocations are appointments of costs to different departments or activities within a company. For instance, overhead costs such as the CEO's salary are often allocated to the company's operating costs. (Berman & Knight, 2013, p.12)
  • Accrurals and allocations are almost always going to be based on assumptions and estimates.
  • Product cost goes into cost of goods sold and if product cost goes up, gross profit (a key measure of product profitability) goes down.
  • Development costs, however, go into research and development, which are included in the operating expense section of the income statement and dont affect gross profit at all.
  • The argument of product and development cost has far reaching implications. If an accountant were to place your wage into development costs for June, rather than the product cost in July, she would be asserting that your work wasnt related to the manufacturing of the product and thus not categorized with product cost. There would be (2) affects.
  • *First, development costs would be larger than they oterwise would be. An executive who analyzes these costs may later on deduce that product development is too expensive, avoiding the action the later, jeopardizing its future.
  • *Second, the product cost is smaller than it would otherwise be. This will affect both pricing and hiring. The product may be priced too low or the company may hire too many workers to put out what appears to be a profitable product.
  • Depreciation also has far reaching implications. Consider, for example, when various airlines found that their planes useful life was lasting longer than initially expected. To reflect this change, accountants lowered the depreciation values from revenue every month.This also had (2) notable effects.
  • Depreciation: Depreciation is the method accountants use to allocate the cost of equipment and other assets to the total cost of products and services as shown on the income statement. It is based on the same idea as accurals: we want to match as closely as possible the costs of our products and services with what we sold. Most capital expenditures are depreciated (land is an example of one that isn't). Accountants attempt to spread the cost of the expenditure over the useful life of the item. (Berman & Knight, 2013, p.14)
  • *First, industry profits rose significantly, reflecting the fact that arilines wouldn't have to be buyign places as soon as they thought.
  • *Second, airline executives thought they could give out better raises.
  • Valuation could be defined as the art of figuring out what a company is worth.
  • *Common textbooks would describe valuation as a company's stock price * no. of shares outstanding, a figure known as the market capitilization (or market cap).
  • *However, a competitor may choose to buy at a premium because the value to them is worth more than it is on the open market - as in the case of a takeover.
  • *For private businesses as well, companies will have to rely on other methods of valuation.
  • Suppose your company is looking at acquiring a company that manufactures industry valves, how would you appraise the company? Would you look at the appraised earnings of similar companies and go to public markets to see how the market values similar companies in relation to their earnings (price-to-earnings ratio method)? Would you view it as purchasing their cash stream (discounted cash flow method)? Would you look at its assets, reputation, or client list (asset valuation method)?
  • Every method of valuation involves assumptions and estimates. For example, for the discounted cash flow method, what would you use to determine the right interest or discount rate for capturing that cash stream?

Chapter 3. Why Increase Your Financial Intelligence?

  • A few years ago, the directors in a sample of Fortune 500 company took a financial literacy test and averaged thirty-two (32) percent.
  • Financial literacy comes with practical benefits:
  • Would you like to know if your employer can make the next payroll?
  • Would you like to know how profitable the products and services you work on actually are?
  • When it comes to your company's capital expenditure ROI analysis, would you like to know if it is based on solid data?
  • With financial literacy, you wil be able to figure these types of questions out.
  • Suppose you worked at the big telecommunications company WorldCom (later known as MCI) during the 1990s. This company had a strategy for acquisition, but had little means of making it happen. As a result, the company had to use stock as its currency, paying for new companies with its share price. Due to this, it had to maintaitn a high stock price and profits. This would not last. When it was uncovered and investigated by the Department of Justice, an inditement was laid out for the various accouning tricks that were used. It would be bankrupt a year later.
  • Similarly, look at Tyco International, a company that purchased ~600 companies in a 2-year period. With this many acquistions bankers began to get nervous with the ever-growing goodwill toward the company on its balance sheet (bankers and investors like to have tangible products on a balance sheet that can be sold off if needed). When rumors started about Tyco having irregular accounting practices, it was stopped from doing further acquisitions.
  • Goodwill: Goodwill comes into play when one company caquires another. It is the difference between the net assets acquired and the amount of money the acquiring company pays for them. For example when a company is purchased for $3 million and only has $1 million in assets, $2 million dollars is recorded in goodwill. This would refer to the company name, reputation, customer list, etc.
  • Balance Sheet: The term balance sheet refers to the assets liabilities and owners' equity at a point in time. That is, it shows, on a specific day, what the company owned, what it owed, and how much it was worth. The balance sheet is called this because it balances - assets must equal liabilities and equity. A financially savvy manager knows that all financial statements flow to the balance sheet.
  • Cash: Cash as presented on the balance sheet means the money a company has in the bank, plus anything else (like stocks and bonds) that can be readily turned into cash.
  • Revenue, as stated previously, is recorded when it is earned, not when a bill is paid. So the top line of the income statement, the line from which we subtract expenses to determine profit, is often no more than a promise. Customers have not paid yet, so the revenue figure often does not reflect real money and neither does the profit line at the bottom.
  • If everything goes well, however, the company will eventually collect its receiveables and will have cash corresponding to the listed profit. In the meantime, it doesn't.
  • This is important to note because if a company is bringing in a lot of revenue, it will want to hire workers but may not be able to pay them at the present time because the revenue is not liquid cash yet.

Chapter 5. Profit is an Estimate

  • It would be correct to say a profitable company is soverign - or able to chart its own course. When a company is not profitable, other people begin to review the company's actions.
  • Profitability is how managers are often judged: Are you contributing to the company's profitability or detracting from it. Are you figuring ways to add profit or not?
  • The fact is profits can, on paper, be skewed. The art of finance might just as easily be termed the art of making a profit - or the art of making profits look better than they actually are.
  • As stated already, this can be done legally and illegally, with most recording legal profit figures.
  • Profit is not the difference of revenues and expenses on an income statement (except if it does cash-based accounting).
  • First, income statements record sales, which as we have stated often is not the same as cash. This figure often takes 30 days to become cash.
  • Second, the cost lines of an income statement are not necessaril the ones it wrote checks for in that period. The costs and expenses on the income statement are those it incurred in generating the sales recorded during that time period. A principle called the matching principle - the appropriate costs should be matched to all sales for the period represented in the income statement.
  • Matching Principle: The matching principle is an accounting rule for income statements. It states to "Match the sale with its associated cost to determine profits in a given period of time."
  • Profit is an estimate due to these types of considerations accountants have to make.
  • In principle, the income statement tries to measue whether the products or services that a company provides are profitable when everthing is added up.
  • Over time, the income statement and the cash flow statement in a well-run company will track one another. Profit will be turned into cash.

Chapter 6. Cracking the Code of the Income Statement

  • Income statement, AKA "profit and loss statement," AKA "P&L statement," AKA "operating statement," AKA "statement of operations," AKA "statement of earnings," AKA "earnings statement."

  • There are different types of income statements. While some have varying periods of time, some are for differnet scopes, like whole businesses or a department in an organization.
  • For large businesses you may see the income statement figures depicted "in millions."
  • Actual income statement: An actual income statement shows what "actually" happened to revenues, costs, and profits during the time period according to the rules of accounting.
  • Pro-forma income statement: A projcted income statement, or what you hope and/or expect to happen.
  • Pro forma could also mean an income statemetn that excludes an usual or one time charges. This could be done to see the effect a one-time charge had on the actual income statement.
  • No matter the income statement, there will be three (3) main parts: Sales or revenue is always at the top. osts and expenses are in the middle, adn profit is at the bottom (occassionally the term may be changed to "surplus/deficiet or "net revenue" for non-profits..
  • You can generally tell what is important by looking at what figure is closest to sales.
  • Consolidated income statements presented in annual reports typically ahve three columns of figures, reflecting what happened during the past three years.
  • Actual % of sales, Budget % of Sales, Variance %
  • Actual previous period, $ Change (+/-), % Change
  • In short these comparative statements are highlighting what is changing, which numbers are where they are supposed to be, and which ones are not.

Chapter 7. Revenue: The Issue Is Recognition

  • Sales: Sales or revenue is the dollar value of all the products or services a company provided to its customers during a period of time.
  • The terms revenue and sales are often the top line of an income statement, however, on occassion you may see the term income - a term that typically refers to the bottom line.
  • A company can recognize revenue when it has earned the revenue, however there is room for interpretation as to when this revenue is earned and thus recorded.
  • The pressure to "manipulate" this data favorably is immense.
  • For example, a company may choose to realize 75% of a given contract right away as, rather than 50%, stating the large figure is due to the large cost of making the sale and initial setup of work.
  • Material changes that impact the bottom line like these should be logged in the footnotes.
  • Earnings per Share: Earnings per Share (EPS) is a company's net profit divided by the number of shares outstanding. It's one of the numbers that Wall Street watches most closely. Wall Street has expectations for many companies EPIS and if the expectations are not met, the share price is likely to drop.
  • The most common source of accounting fraud has always been and probably will always be the top line: sales.
  • One example of how this is done is called channel stuffing, where a company sends unordered product to a distributor and recognizes the sale.

Chapter 8. Costs and Expenses: No Hard-and-Fast Rules

  • Costs and expenses fall into two basic categories:
  • The first expense is Cost of Goods sold (COGS), or Costs of Services (COS)
  • Costs of Goods Sold or Cost of Services includes all the costs direcly involved in producing a product or delievering a service.
  • This would include (for a production plant) the wages of the staff or the price of the material used.
  • It would create a grey area for other areas like: the salary price of the manager who oversees the work, the wages of the supervisor, sales commissions.
  • GAAP does not give concrete details about how it wants these metrics recorded, only giving guidelines.
  • GAAP, or generally accepted accounting principles, defines the standard for creating financial reports in hte United States. It helps to ensure the validity and reliability of financial statements, and allows for wasy comparison between companies and across industries. GAAP does not spell out everything. It allowws for a lot of discrecretion and judgment calls.
  • What is listed in the Cost of Goods Sold ledger is important.
  • Consider, for example, a scenario where you run an archtectural firm and in the past your staff's salaries have been included in COS.Now finance is saying it is moving your salary and other administrative salary out of COS.This is fine because you are not direcly involved in the service. You are now no longer "above the line." This would make your gross profit higher and may let off some attention even though there were no real changes.
  • Consider, for example, a scenario where you are a plant manager charged with making a gross profit of $1 million a month. This month you are $20,000 short. You then realize $25,000 of COGS is a line item called "contract administration on plant orders." Does this really belong in COGS? You petition the controller to move those costs to operatin gexpendses. Your controller agrees and the change is done. You hit your taget and everyone is happy. Outsiders may look and believe you are improving when, again, nothing changed except for recording practices.
  • Above the Line, Below the Line: the "line" generally refers to the gross profit. Above that line on the income statement typically are sales and COGS or COS. Below the line are operting expenses, interest and taxes. What is the difference. Items listed above the line tend to vary move( in the short term) than many of those below the line. and so tend to get more managerial attention.
  • Both of these changes are legal, so long as they meet the reasonably and consistent test. You can even take an expense out of COGS one month and put it back the next month. All you need is a good reason to convince your controller (and subsequent auditor) the changes are material to the company financials.
  • The second pat of expenses is operating expenses, referred to as being "below the line."
  • Some companies may refer to this costs as sales, administrative, and general expenses (SG&A or just G&A), while others may treat G&A as a subcategory and gives sales and marketing its own line.
  • Sometimes these categories may be split due to the size of the company or cost value itself.
  • Either way, operating cost is commonly known as "overhead" and includes costs such as rent, utilities, telephone, research, and marketing. It also includes management and staff staries -HR, accounting, IT, items that the controller does not believe go in COGS, etc.
  • Operating expenses are those costs that are not direclyrelated to the making the product or delivering a sercice.
  • One might assume that COGS would be variable costs and operating expenses are fixed costs, but that is not always the case, as is the case when you have a commissioned sales force.
  • Depreciationa and amortization are also typically recorded in operating expenses and also has room for manipulation.
  • Consider, for example, a scenario where you are the accountant for a delivery company and purchase a truck for $36,000. If you assume the truck to last 3 years, yo would depreciate it at 1,000/mo yielding net profit 1,000. If you believe it will last one year, you may depreciate it at 3000/mo, yielding net loss of 1000. If you believe it willlast 6 years, it will depreciate 500/mo, yeidlgin a net profit of 1500. In one case our net profit goes into the red, whereas in the other oru net profit increases by 50%.

Sometimes this action is done for restructuring but in this case WMI was doing this because it had been cooking its books on an unimaginable scale. These cost money to acquire and have a value, yet are not physical like a truck. Your company ahd to buy the patent and/or do the research that lies behind it and then apply for it.

  • Noncash expense: A noncash expense is one that is charged to a period of the income statement but is not acutall paid in cash . An example is depreciation, accountatns deduct a certain amount each month for depreciation of equipment but hte company isnt obligated to pay out that amount because the equipmetn was acquired in a previous period.

Chapter 9. The Many Forms of Profit

  • Revenue minus costs and expenses equals profit - or net income, or earnings, or net margin.
  • Either way, profit is defined as the amount left over after expenses are subtracted from revenue. There are three basic types of profit: gross profit, operating profit and net profit. Each one is determined by subtracting various expenses from revenue.
  • Gross profit is defined as revenue minus cost of goods sold or cost of services. It is what is left over after a company has paid all of its direct costs incurred for making a product or delivering a service. Gross profit must be sufficient to cover a business; operating expenses, taxes, financial costs, and net profit.
  • Operating profit, or EBIT, is gross profit minus operating expenses, which include depreciation and amortization. In other words it shows what the profit from running the business.
  • Net profit is the bottom line of an income statement. It is what is left after all costs and expenses are subtracted from revenue. Its operating profit minus interest expenses, taxes, one-time charges and any other costs not included in the operating profit.
  • What constitutes a "healthy" profit varies by industry and is likely to vary from one company to another even in the same industry. A gross profit rate for a grocery chain is likely to be smaller than one that sells jewlery. Other things being equal, a company with larger revenues can thrive with a lower gross percentage than a smaller one - explaining why Walmart can charge such low prices..
  • To gauge your company's gross profit you can compare it to industry standards. You can also look at year-to-year trends, examining whether your gross profit is headed up or down, and if down, asky why.It is also important to remember that gross profit is subject to bias based on how you choose to recognize revenue and by the decisions regarding COGS.
  • Consider, for example, you are an HR director for a market research firm and you find gross profit is decreasing. After looking at the nubmers you find that service costs have gone up, so you and your team start strategizing how to lower those costs, even discussing layoffs. Then you find that salaries that were previously in operating expenseshave been moved into COGS. So service costs did not go up, adn laying off people would be a mistake. Now you have to talk with your staff in accounting. You would now need to explain the change and thus why the old metrics of gross profit may need to be adjusted.
  • Operating profit, or EBIT, is the profit from operations. Taxes are not included b/c they do not have anything to do with how you are running your company. Additionally, interest expenses depend on whether the company is financed with debt or equity.
  • But the financial structure of the company doesnt say anything about how well it is run from an operational perspective. Rather operating profit is a good gauge of how well a company is being managed. It is watched to show both how myuch demand their is for the company's products and services as well as how efficient the company is at delivering those goods. A healthy and growing operating profit suggests that the employees are going to to be able to kjeep their jobs and may have opportuinities for advancement.
  • The bias for operating costs generally would occur in the form of onetime charges, the depreciation line. Due to the abuse and fraud that has occurred in teh past, many companies now look at EBITDA, or earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization as a better measure of operational efficientc because it ginored noncash charges such as depreciation altogher.
  • Net profit, or the bottom line, is usually the last line on the income statement.
  • Many of the the key numbers used ot measure a company, such as earnings per share and price/earnings ratio, are based on net profit.
  • When net profit is low, and moneying with the books is ruled out, there are only 3 poissible fixes for low profitability.
  • One, the company can increase profitable sales.
  • Two, it can lower production costs and run more efficiently - that is reduce COGS.
  • Three, it can cut operating expenses.
  • Options one and two both take a long amount of time and so the third option is generally favored for short term solution available and almost always occurs in the form of layofs. The negative impact of this action include: damage to morale, good emploees start lookign for other roles, and if the share price increases to a premium the stock buyers may purchase anticipating it to go up meaning that no new staff is hired causing the staff to limp along.
  • Point of the chapter: For most companies it is better to manage fothe long faul and focus on increaseing profitable sales and reducing costs. Operating expenses may have to be truimmed but if that is your ownly focus you are likely only delaying a day of reckoning.

Chapter 10. Understanding Balance Sheet Basics

  • If you give your financials to an experienced manager in a business, he will liekly turn to the income statement. If you give your financials to a banker or experienced Wall Street investor, or maybe a veteran board member, he will likely turn to the balance sheet.
  • Why dont the managers do waht the pros do? There are (3) main reasons:
  • The balance sheet is harder to get your mind around than the income statement.
  • Most companies' budgeting processes focus on revenue and expenses. In other words the budget categories more or less align with the income statement. You can t be a manger withuot knowing somehting about budgeting - which auomatically means that you are familiar with many of the lines on the income statemnt. Balance sheet data by contrast rarely figures ina n income operating managers budgeting process (although the finance dept. certianly budgets the balance sheet accounts).
  • Three, Managing the balance sheet requires a deeper understanding of finance than manageing an oncome statement. You not only have to know what the various categories refer to you have to know how they fit together. You also hacve to understand how changes in the balance sheet impact tghe other financeial stamements and vice versa.
  • To be clear, a balance sheet is a statement of what a company owns and what is owes at a certian point in time. The difference between these two factors is called equity.
  • Equity, also called book value, is the shareholders "stake" in the company as measured by accounting rules. In accounting terms, equity will always be assets minus liabilities.It is also the sum of all capital paid in by shareholders plus any profits earned by the company since its inception minus divideds paid out to shareholders.
  • Just as one of a company's goals is to increase profitability anotehr is to increase equity.
  • If profitability is your grade for a certain class in college, equity would be your overall GPA, as it reflects your overall performance. Over time, the equity section of the balance sheet shows the accumulation of profits or losses left in the business, the line that is called retained earnings (losses) or sometimes accumulated earnings (deficiet).
  • When we calculate our own value, sometimes we talk about net worth. (Net worth = owns - owes. In a similar way, we can owns = owest + net worth.
  • For an individual this "ownership" category is made of cash in the bank, big items like a house, and all other items the person lays claim to.
  • For the same individual, the "owes" category is made of a mortgage, credit card debt, and other debt.
  • Companies operate similarly, but use different languadge:
  • Assets are what a company owns
  • Liabilities are what a company owes
  • Equity is what the company is worth.
  • Like income statements, these are generally used to determine a point in time, usually a month, quarter, or fiscal year.
  • Fiscal year: A fiscal year is a 12 month period that a company uses for accounting purposes. Many companies use the calandar year, but some use other periods. Some retailers use a specific weekend, such as the last sunday of the year to mark the end of their fiscal year. ou must know the company's fiscal year to ascetrain how recent the information you are looking at is.
  • Unlike income statements, balance sheets are generally for a whole organization, although sometimes a large corporation creates subsidiar balance sheets for its operating divisiosn.
  • As with other financial elements, the balance sheet involves making assumptions and estimates (how to depreciate trucks).
  • There are 2 main ways that balance sheets are dispayed:
  • The traditional model shows assets on the left hand side of the page and liaibilties and equity on the right hand side of the page.

Chapter 11. Assets: More Estimates and Assumptions (Except for Cash)

  • Assets are what a company owns.
  • Assets include: cash and securities, machinery and equipment, buildings and land, whatever.
  • Current assets, which ususally come first on a balance sheet, include anything that can become cash in less than a year.
  • Long-term assets are those that have a useful life of more than a year.
  • Cash and cash equivalents refer to liquid and readily liquid cash.
  • Easily cashable stocks and bonds would be in this group, as they can be liquidated in <24 hours.
  • When Microsoft says it has 56 billion dollars in cash and short term investments, it means it has that much in the bank, monetary funds, and publicly traded securities.
  • Accounts receiveable refers to the amount owed to the company by customers.
  • This figure is comprised of promises to pay, that have yet to be collected.
  • This is an asset b/c these commitments will convert to cash soon and belong to the company.
  • Some balance sheets includes an item called "allowance for bad debt." This refers to the estimated figure of customers who will not pay.
  • Many companies use this tool of "bad debt" as a reason to smooth their earnings as well.
  • "Smoothing Earnings:" Smoothing earnings refers to the act of adding charges for the purpose of maintaining steady and predictable growth. This is utilized b/c unforseen and unexplained spikes in financial reports is viewed negatively, as it is often a sign that management is not in control of a business.
  • When you increase the bad debt reserve on a balance sheet, you have to strenghten an expense against profit on the income statement.
  • When you decrease the bad debt reserve on a balance sheet, you have to lessen an expense against profit on the income statement.
  • Again, there is room for subjectivity when making estimates on this figure.
  • Service companies typically do not have much in terms of inventory, but most other industries like manufacturers, wholesalers retailers.
  • One part of the inventory figure is finding out the value of products that are ready ot bve sold. This is referred to as finished goods.
  • Another segment of inentory concerns products that are under construction, whcih are referred to as works in progress, (WIP).
  • A third segment of inventory concerns the raw matericals that make up the products after undergoing value-adding activities. This is called "raw materials."
  • Accountants have to make judgment calls on what the value of the second and third type of inventory should be recorded as, for example under a FIFO (first-in,first-out) or LIFO (last-in, first-out) method.
  • Many would agree that having a limited inventory while covering business activity is ideal as this improves the cash level of the business
  • Property, plant, and equipment (or PPE) on the balance sheet will refer to buildings, equipment, machinery, trucks, computers and every other physical asset a comapny owns.
  • The PPE figure is the total number of dolars it cost to buy all the facilities andequipmetn the company uses to operate the business.
  • Note htat the relevant cost here is he pruchase price, which is recorded at cost otherwise constant appraisals would be needed and further bias would potentially occur.
  • This includes land, so land 30 years ago that was purchased for $500k would still be recorded as such despite being worth vastly more today.
  • Unlike land, buildings do depreciate each year. The point of accounting depreciation isnt, however, to estimate what buldings are worth rigth now though. Rather the point is to allocate the investment in the asset over the time it is used to generate revenue and profits (matching principle). In this way the depreciation charge serves to acurately reflect the true cost of goods sold.
  • To calculte the accumulated depreciation, accountants add up all the charges for depreciation that have taken since the day an asset was bought.
  • As was the case on the incoem statemetn, this figure can be biased as well with its depreciation metrics, if one wer to halve the depreciation profits would go up accordingly.
  • Goodwill as covered is the difference between the price paid for a company and the net assets the acquirer actually gets.
  • Buying a company with a large amount of goodwill isnt necessarily a bad deal, as it would include the name, client list, knowledgeable employees, and other intengible assets.
  • In the past, goodwill could have been amortized, however FASB has since decided that goodwill does not lose value overtime. The effect of this change is that companies look even harder to purchase goodwill to beef up their perceived profits.
  • In the past, goodwill was ideal b/c it would be amortized over a longer period than physical assets are depreciated at. Under the new rule, with amortization not occuring, this argument is strengthened b/c it does not affect profit figures.
  • Intellectual property, patents, and other intengibles are, however, amortized over the life of their revenue streams..
  • By the same token, research and development costs that do not result in an asset that is likley to generate revenue should be recorded as an expense.
  • This creates an opportunity for bias. GAAP states that R&D can be amortized if the product under development is technologically feasible. Under this criteria, costs can be amortized over long periods making proits look better - otherwise R&D needs to be recordced as expenses when they are incurred.
  • Accruals and prepaid assets are also logged as assets.
  • Consider for example, you own a bicycle manufacturing company. You prepay the rent for the first year, with $5,000 of $60,000 going to first months rent under the matching principle. In this scenario, the extra $55,000 would go under prepaid rent to keep track of the asset and counter charge on the balance sheet.
  • Under this scenario, every month, you would take $5,000 from your prepaid asset line on your balance sheet assets and place it in your income statement as an expense for rent.
  • This act is an accrual, and the account on the balance sheet that records what has not yet been expensesd is called an accrued asset account.
  • This too presents an opportunity for bias.
  • Consider, for example, that your company launches a $1 million dolalr advertisement campaign. Because of the cost you decide it might last for (2) years so you amortize the cost over 24 months. A company may be incventivized to record the expense all at once if there is a great first month though b/c it could hurt profits later to keep the amortization rate. In this second model, the amortization rate would be cleared and no risk to future reported profits.

Chapter 12. On the Other Side: Liabilities and Equity

  • Liabilities, or what a company owes, can also be viewed as a way in which a company gained its assets.
  • Debt as opposed to equity.
  • Current liabilites refers to those that have to be paid off in less than a year.
  • Long-term liabilities are those that are due in over a year.
  • Liabilities are ususally organized in terms of when they are due on the balance sheet, with shortest-term items appearing closer to the top.
  • There are several terms within the realm of liabilites that are noteworthy:
  • Current Portion of Long-Term Debt: refers to the subset of long-term debt that is due within 30 days. (ex: If you owe $100,000 in a loan over a year, but $10k within 30 days, $10k will go in current liabilities under a line item called "Current Portion of Long-Term Debt" (or equivalent), whereas 90k will go into long term liaibilites.
  • Short-term loans: refers to lines of credit and short-term revolving loans. These short term credit lines are usually securited by current assets such as accounts receiveable and inventory. The entire balance is shown here.
  • Accounts Payable refers: a line item on financial statements that shows the amount a company owes its vendors. The company receives goods and servieces from suppliers and doesnt typically pay the bill for at least 30 days.
  • Accrued expenses and other short term liabilities refers to a catch all category that includes evertyhing else the company owes. One example is payroll, where accountants would estimate how muh the company owest you on octoer 1 for work completed in september and charge those expenses to september. This is an accruied liabilites. It is like an internal bill in deptermber to be made in october accrued liabilities are part of the matching principle. we ahve to match ezxpesnses with the revenue they help to bring in every month.
  • Long-Term Liabilities: Most long-term liabilities are lons. But there are others you may see listed here. Examples include deferred bonuses or compensation, deferred taxes, and pension libibilities. If these other liabilities are substancial, this section needs to be monitored closely.
  • Onwers equity: Onwers; equity is waht is left after we subtract liaibilities from assets. It includes capital provided by investors and the profits retained by the company over time. It goes by many names including sharholder equity and stockholder equity.
  • Onwers' equity generally includes the following categories:
  • Preferred shares: refers to a specific type of stock. Holders of this stock receive their dividends before holders of common stock. The downside of this stock lies in that these types of stocks: carry a fixed dividend (and thus do not receive the full benefit in a companys growth value) and do not incur voting rights. The difference between this stock and a bond is in that bonds get fixed interest payment whereas preferred stocks get a fixed dividend. Companies issue this stock instead of bonds b/c it does not carry the same implications as debt. Bondholders can force a company that has not paid into bankruptcy while preferred stockholders normally cant.
  • Common shares: refers to a specicic type of stock as well. Holders of this stock may or may not receive dividends. Additionally, this type of stock carries voting rights.The value shown on the balance sheet is the nominal dollar amount assigned to the stock by the issuer. Par value is usually a small amount has has no relationship to the stocks market price.
  • Additional Paid-In Capital: refers to the amount over par that investors paid for a stock. For example, if the stock initially sold at $5 per share and par value is $1, then additional paid in capital is $4.This figure is summed up over time so if a company issues additional shares the additional paid in capital is added to the exsisiting amount.
  • Retained Earnings: refers to the profits that have been reinvested in the business instead of being paid out in dividends. The figure represents the total after tax income that has been reinvested ro retained over the life of the business. Sometimes this figure is held in cash (which stockholders want in productive assets or as dividends).
  • Owners equity does not mean a figure that would be paid out to the owners if the buisness were sold, becuase of all the estimates and assumptions. The figure that could be paid out would be the market cap - or shares*share price on a given day.

Chapter 13. Why the Balance Sheet Balances

  • The balance sheet will always show assets, liabilities, and owners' equity.
  • The assets will almost alwys be on the left and liabilities and owners equity will be on the right.
  • Since you never get anything for nothing, the owns side and the how we got it side will always be in balance.
  • Consider, for example, a new company that the owner has invested 50,000 in. The company will have 50,000 in assets and the owners equity will be 50,000.
  • Now consider if the company purchased a 36000 truck. Under this scenario, cash would decrease to 14,000 as a result of the expense and a truck would be recorded in assets with owners equity staying 50,000.
  • Now consider if the owner gets a 10,000 loan from a bank, cash increases to 24,000 and liabilities goes to 10,000 to compensate.
  • To summarize, every transaction that affects one side of the balance sheet affects both sides of the balance sheet.
  • Remember if you do not have assets equaling liabilities plus owners equity, you do not have a balance sheet.

Chapter 14. The Income Statement Affects the Balance Sheet

  • A change in one statement nearly always has an impact on the other statements.
  • When you are managing the income statement, expect an effect on the balance sheet.
  • To see the relationship between profit from the income statement and equity, which appears on the balnce sheet.
  • As you conduct business activities you spend casn (current asset, owners' equity) on raw materials and labor to recognize a new accounts receiveable balance (current asset, and owners equity).
  • That timeframe's net profit becomes owners equity, which falls under retained earnings. Also any loans incurrerrerd also go on the income stament as well as the balance sheet.
  • Additionally, there is an obserable relationship between COGS and operating expenses on the income statement and the balance sheet as well.
  • As you purchase inventory (current asset), it is recorded as an expense under COGS on the income statement, impacting both net profit and owners' equity.
  • When inventory is sold, the associated cost is moved from COGS (income statement) to reduce inventory (current asset), directly influencing the balance sheet as well as net profit.
  • Point of the chapter: Investors can learn a lot from looking at a company's balance sheet for even a moment. Questions like "Is owners equity rising, and if so is it b/c of a infusino of capital or is it b/c the company has been making money?"
